Effects of COVID-19 on the Town of Cary (March 2020 - May 2021)

COVID-19 in North Carolina

Everyone has a story about when the pandemic began to feel “real” – an event cancellation, an all-staff email from their boss, their child’s school going virtual.

For many, it had already hit home by the time Governor Roy Cooper issued Executive Order 121 on March 27, 2020, implementing a statewide Stay at Home Order set to begin three days later on Monday, March 30. The order directed North Carolinians to limit trips outside the home for all but a few reasons (visiting essential businesses, exercising outdoors, or helping a family member). In many peoples’ memories, the ensuing months are a blur -- mask mandates enacted, rescinded, and enacted again; restaurants turning to curbside and delivery service to stay afloat; businesses struggling to adapt amid widespread uncertainty; and a dawning realization that we’d all be living with COVID, in some form, for a very long time.





For a government entity, the story of the pandemic has been, at least in part, one of metrics. To get a sense of how COVID affected (and continues to affect) Cary citizens, we look to data. Of course, data can’t tell the whole story, but it can point to trends, support or refute hypotheses, and give us an idea of where our services might be needed as the pandemic continues through its second year.

Because the pandemic continues to affect day-to-day life in our area and around the country, data for this story focuses on the period from March 2020 to May 2021, and does not account for the summer 2021 surge in delta variant cases.

With all that said, let’s examine a few statistics to quantify how life changed for Cary citizens and the police who serve them.

Cary's police incidents and car crashes drastically decreased

During the COVID-19 pandemic some striking trends in our data began to emerge. Specifically, our police and crash incident data showed drastic decreases, especially during the beginning of the pandemic.

May 2020 saw just 335 total incidents reported to police, the lowest monthly number since September 2018. Remarkably, police incidents would remain below 400 for eight consecutive months. Towards the end of 2020 and into the beginning of 2021, we saw an increase in incidents, however total incidents pre-pandemic (January-March 2020) were still about 13.41% higher than January-March 2021. While everyone was at home, the roads were noticeably safer, with April 2020 seeing only 148 reported crashes. Prior to that month, the next lowest month in nearly 4.5 years was July 2017 with 312 crashes. So even the safest pre-pandemic month in recent memory had nearly 111% more traffic collisions than the first full month of the pandemic. Throughout the remainder of 2020, the monthly number of reported crashes fluctuated but never went above 277. Cary's Greenway usage dramatically increased

An interesting byproduct of North Carolina’s Stay at Home order was a dramatic rise in visits to the few places people could go: outdoor public spaces. As local and state emergency orders began to take effect, we saw a drastic increase in visits to our greenway system. Using Smart City technology, Cary collects and monitors greenway usage data via pedestrian counters (Eco-Counters).

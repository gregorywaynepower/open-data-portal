Overview

Today, most appliances are labeled as “high-efficiency” but it wasn’t always that way.

Cary’s conservation strategy began in 1996 with an ambitious goal at a time of extraordinary growth of reducing per person usage by 20% by 2015. The vision to was to support the high quality of life in Cary by continuing to provide safe, and reliable water service, but also to steward well both our natural resources and the infrastructure investments.

That goal was met earlier than expected, achieving a 20% reduction by 2013. Remarkably, the conservation trend has continued with a reduction of 30% by 2023. Additionally, thanks to a steady reduction in maximum day demand, Cary was able to delay the expansion of two water treatment facilities, resulting in significant cost savings.

The journey included impressive community support, multiple droughts, cutting-edge technology, and significant shifts in both industry and development standards.

This story honors the history, challenges and successes of Cary’s water conservation strategy and the way it continues to shape Cary today.

The Challenge - 1990s

As more people moved to Cary in the suburban boom of the early 1990s. Many of the single family homes came with irrigation systems installed to water lawns with thirsty fescue turf. A large and green lawn became a social normal. This expectation resulted in a dramatic increase in water demand, especially on hot summer days.

Water Treatment and Reclamation Facilities are designed to handle peak day demand which means irrigation was driving the time and size of projected infrastructure needs. As Cary was planning for the future water system, it became clear that partnering with the community to reduce per capita usage was going to be necessary to sustainably steward our shared resources.

The Goal - 1996

Cary adopted a water conservation program in 1996 with the goal to reduce daily water usage per person by 20% in 20 years, measured as GPCD (gallons per capita per day).

That goal was ambitious but well-supported with a comprehensive conservation strategy including regulations, education and incentives.

The conservation strategy had 3 main objectives:

  • Support the high quality of life in Cary by providing safe, reliable water service, while reducing the per capita use of water
  • Conserve a natural resource
  • Reduce cost of infrastructure expansion

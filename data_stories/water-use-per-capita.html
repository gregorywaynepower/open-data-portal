Overview

While most of today’s appliances are labeled as “high-efficiency,” it wasn’t always that way.

Cary’s conservation strategy began in 1996, a time of extraordinary growth for the town, with the ambitious goal of reducing per person water usage by 20% by 2015. The vision was to support the high quality of life in Cary by continuing to provide safe and reliable water service, but also to steward our natural resources and infrastructure investments.

That goal was met earlier than expected, with the 20% reduction mark hit by 2013. Remarkably, this conservation trend has continued with a reduction of 30% by 2023. Additionally, thanks to a steady reduction in maximum day demand, Cary was able to realize significant cost savings.

Thanks to impressive community support, this successful journey was a success included multiple droughts, cutting-edge technology, and significant shifts in both industry and development standards during its span.

This story honors the history, challenges, and successes of Cary's water conservation strategy and the way it continues to shape Cary today.

The Challenge: 1990s

As more people moved to Cary during the suburban boom of the early 1990s, many new single family homes included irrigation systems installed to water lawns with thirsty fescue turf. A large, green lawn became a social normal. This expectation resulted in a dramatic increase in water demand, especially on hot summer days.

Water Treatment and Reclamation Facilities are sized to handle peak day demand which means irrigation was driving the time and size of projected infrastructure needs. As Cary was planning for the future water system, it became clear that partnering with the community to reduce per capita usage was going to be necessary to sustainably steward our shared resources.

The Goal: 1996

Cary adopted a water conservation program in 1996 with the goal to reduce daily water usage per person by 20% in 20 years, measured as gallons per capita per day (GPCD).

That goal was ambitious but well-supported with a comprehensive conservation strategy that included regulations, education, and incentives.

The conservation strategy had these main objectives:

  • Support the high quality of life in Cary by providing safe, reliable water service, while reducing the per capita use of water
  • Conserve a natural resource
  • Reduce the impact of future infrastructure expansion

The Strategy: Late 90s, 2000s

With nearly 40 years of conservation history, we can't explore it all, but this story will share some of the highlights along the journey. The program was based on leading national programs in the Southwest and California. The comprehensive program grew quickly and adapted as needed. At its peak it, was directly administered by as many as six staff members.

Here are some of the key tactics within the three main approaches.

Regulatory

  • 1997 - Water Waste Ordinance & Rain Sensor Ordinance
  • 2000 - Alternate Day Watering Ordinance
  • 2003 - Irrigation System Design Requirements
  • 2003 - Drought-Tolerant Plant Requirement (non-residential customers)

Educational

  • 1997 - Beat the Peak Campaign Begins
  • 1998 - Block Leader Program & Residential Audits Begin
  • 1999 - School Programs & WaterWise Workshops Begin

Financial Incentives

  • 1997 - Toilet Flapper Rebate Program, Give-Aways (efficient showerheads, faucet aerators, 5-minute shower timers, rain gauges)
  • 1998 - Tiered Rate Structure
  • 2001/2008 - Rain Barrels at cost/Build-Your-Own Rain
  • 2008 - Turf Buy Back Program; High Efficiency Toilet Refit Rebate Program

Smart Meters - 2011

While the term Smart City is becoming more common every day, Cary led the trend by installing Smart Water Meters system-wide in 2011 when the technology was still cutting edge. The Customer Portal allows residents and business-owners to view hourly, daily and weekly consumption online.

This granularity allows for detection of high-usage and potential leaks within a day which previously would have gone unidentified for over a month.

This technology allows individuals to set high usage alerts and actively manage their water use, empowering them with the insight needed to optimize conservation.

Unprecedented Droughts

No one could have foreseen the record-breaking regional droughts around the corner but Cary’s strong conservation strategy laid the firm foundation necessary to weather the storm. The most severe droughts in recent history here occurred during 2001-2002 and 2007-2008, summarized by the State Climate Office.

The 2007-2008 drought caused significant regional attention. Many citizens voluntarily changed their habits (both indoor and outdoor) in ways that continue today.

While Jordan Lake, Cary’s water supply, was stable, Cary briefly entered into Stage 1 of our water shortage response plan out of regional solidarity.

The regional strain spurred the development of regional agreements and infrastructure to be able to support one another in times of extreme need. The Triangle Water Supply Partnership continued to promote this spirit of cooperation and interconnectedness.

The Summary & the Success

Cary’s water conservation strategy became nationally recognized for its comprehensiveness and success. While sudden growth initially spurred this effort, the framework allows Cary to serve as a leader during times of regional drought.

The success was a result of partnership from both individuals and the industry.

After the drought, there was more societal appreciation and acceptance for warm-season turf. Additionally, with changing development patterns, irrigation systems no longer became standard fixtures in new homes. The irrigation industry advanced more efficient technologies such as soil moisture and rain sensors and urged industry professionals to design more efficient layouts.

Today, many new homes have significantly smaller yards on average, further reducing irrigation demand. Also, high-efficiency appliances have become standard for both new and older homes.

Along with behavioral changes, all of these factors have contributed greatly to a reduction in per capita usage.

As for the goal? We blew it out of the water with a 20% reduction realized years earlier than expected and over 30% reduction to date.

Looking Forward: Your Choices and Voice Matter

The initial goals of the water conservation strategy are still valid and important today:

  • Support the high quality of life in Cary by providing safe, reliable water serfice, while reducing the per capita use of water
  • Conserve a natural resource
  • Reduce the impact of future infrastructure expansion

This means, while great progress has been made, everyone is needed to do their part to use water wisely.

Check out Cary’s water conservation page to see how you can track your water usage, get tips on how to fix a leak or optimize your irrigation plans.

Today, Cary’s role as a regional environmental leader ranges from conservation to composting, from transportation to trees, and water to energy. You can learn more about our stewardship strategies and our Count Me In, Cary! survey.

As we continue to build a resilient and sustainable community, know that Cary has a long history of successful trail-blazing.

WaterSense

Since its inception in 2006, Cary has been a partner with WaterSense, an outreach branch of the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) providing high-quality education and outreach materials related to water efficiency. Products with a WaterSense Label offer a simple way for consumers to identify water-efficient products by ensuring that they meet the EPA's specifications for performance. You can also find resources for both kids and adults on the WaterSense home page.
